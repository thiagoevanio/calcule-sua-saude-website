Omega-3:

Clinical Pearl

The American Heart Association recommends consuming fatty fish (salmon, sardines, tuna) at least twice a week as the primary source of omega-3 fatty acids, before resorting to capsule supplementation. Whole foods provide synergistic nutrients — vitamin D, selenium, and protein — that amplify the benefits of omega-3 fatty acids.

1. What Are Omega-3 Fatty Acids?

Omega-3 fatty acids are long-chain polyunsaturated fatty acids (LC-PUFA n-3) classified as essential nutrients, as the human body cannot produce them in sufficient quantities to meet physiological demands. There are three primary forms with distinct origins and biological properties that determine their clinical relevance.

EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid) and DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) are found primarily in cold-water fish, seafood, and microalgae. They are the biologically active forms responsible for the majority of documented benefits in the scientific literature. DHA is structural, composing cell membranes in the brain and retina, while EPA plays a predominantly anti-inflammatory and cardioprotective role.

ALA (alpha-linolenic acid) is of plant origin, abundant in chia seeds, flaxseeds, and walnuts. While it is a precursor to EPA and DHA, metabolic conversion in the body is limited — generally less than 10% for EPA and under 1% for DHA — making marine sources irreplaceable for populations with high physiological demand, such as pregnant women and the elderly.

2. Anti-inflammatory Mechanisms

Chronic low-grade inflammation underlies cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, obesity, Alzheimer's disease, and numerous cancers. Omega-3 fatty acids act at multiple points in this inflammatory cascade, exerting effects that go far beyond simple lipid modulation.

The central mechanism involves competition with arachidonic acid (omega-6, AA) for the enzymes cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase (LOX). When EPA and DHA compete with AA for these enzymes, production of pro-inflammatory eicosanoids is significantly reduced: series-2 prostaglandins (PGE2), thromboxanes (TXA2), and series-4 leukotrienes (LTB4) are displaced by series-3 and series-5 derivatives, which are biologically less potent.

Furthermore, EPA and DHA are precursors to specialized pro-resolving mediators (SPMs): resolvins (RvE1 from EPA; RvD1 from DHA), protectins (PD1/NPD1), and maresins. These mediators do not merely passively suppress inflammation — they actively promote its resolution, stimulating cellular debris removal and tissue restoration.

DHA also generates Protectin D1 (NPD1), which exerts neuroprotection by inhibiting pro-inflammatory Bcl-2-mediated neuronal apoptosis. At the genomic level, EPA and DHA suppress the transcription factor NF-κB, reducing expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-6, TNF-α, and IL-1β — the same ones that elevate high-sensitivity CRP and cardiovascular risk.

3. Cardiovascular Health

The most robust evidence for omega-3 focuses on cardiometabolic health. The most reproducible and largest-magnitude effect is reduction of plasma triglycerides: doses of 2 to 4g of EPA+DHA per day reduce levels by 20 to 50%, with efficacy proportional to baseline levels. This effect is so consistent that the FDA approved icosapent ethyl (IPE, pure EPA) — marketed as Vascepa — for the treatment of severe hypertriglyceridemia.

The REDUCE-IT trial (Bhatt et al., NEJM 2018), with over 8,000 patients in secondary cardiovascular prevention with elevated triglycerides, demonstrated that IPE at 4g/day reduced major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) by 25% compared to mineral placebo — a result that pleasantly surprised the cardiology community.

Meta-analyses of over 70 randomized controlled trials (RCTs) documented a modest but significant reduction in blood pressure: approximately 1.5 mmHg in systolic and 1.0 mmHg in diastolic, with a dose-dependent effect. The mechanism involves increased endothelial nitric oxide synthesis, reduced vascular resistance, and improved endothelial function.

Anti-arrhythmic effects derive from the incorporation of EPA and DHA into cardiomyocyte membranes, which alters transmembrane ionic currents and reduces abnormal electrical excitability — a mechanism relevant to the prevention of ventricular fibrillation. Additionally, there is reduced platelet aggregation and blood viscosity, contributing to a more favorable hemostatic profile.

It is important to contextualize: trials such as ASCEND (diabetic patients without established CVD) and VITAL (general population) showed more modest benefits, suggesting that the impact of omega-3 is more pronounced in high-risk populations — especially those with elevated triglycerides and prior cardiovascular disease.

"Long-chain omega-3 fatty acids are unique among nutrients in the breadth of their biological actions — simultaneously modulating lipids, inflammation, endothelial function, and cardiac electrophysiology."

4. Brain and Cognitive Health

The human brain is an extraordinarily lipid-rich organ: approximately 60% of its dry mass is composed of fats, and DHA represents about 40% of all brain PUFAs. This abundance is not coincidental — DHA is fundamental to neuronal membrane fluidity, myelin sheath formation, and synaptic efficiency.

During the third trimester of pregnancy and the first years of life, DHA is actively transferred from mother to fetus and infant, being critical for the development of brain architecture and the retina. Deficits during this period are associated with persistent cognitive and visual impairment, which justifies specific supplementation during pregnancy and breastfeeding.

In the context of mental health, high-quality meta-analyses (Grosso et al., 2014; Liao et al., 2019) confirm that EPA supplementation — especially in proportions greater than 60% of the formulation — exerts a significant antidepressant effect compared to placebo, with a modest to moderate effect size. The effective dose for adjunctive treatment in major depressive disorder is between 1 and 2g of EPA/day.

Regarding prevention of dementia and Alzheimer's disease, prospective observational studies consistently associate higher lifelong DHA intake with lower incidence of AD and slower cognitive decline. Interventional clinical trials, however, show mixed results — possibly because the benefit is greater in primary prevention and early stages, before the establishment of amyloid pathology. The erythrocyte omega-3 index (target ≥8%) emerges as a promising biomarker of cognitive risk.

5. Other Clinical Applications

Scientific evidence for omega-3 extends well beyond heart and brain health, encompassing a spectrum of inflammatory and metabolic conditions:

  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Randomized clinical trials demonstrate that EPA+DHA (≥2.7g/day) reduces morning stiffness, joint pain, and the need for non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), with increasing benefit after 3 months of continuous use.
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD): EPA+DHA supplementation reduces hepatic steatosis, ALT/AST levels, and markers of hepatic inflammation, as documented by meta-analyses with ultrasound and biopsy assessment.
  • Pregnancy and perinatal outcomes: The Cochrane review by Middleton et al. (2018), including 70 RCTs with over 19,000 women, demonstrated that omega-3 supplementation during pregnancy reduces the risk of preterm birth (<37 weeks) by 11% and very preterm birth (<34 weeks) by 42%. The current recommendation is 200mg of additional DHA/day for pregnant women.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease: Beneficial effects in Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis, particularly in reducing fecal inflammatory markers and maintaining remission.
  • Oncological protection: Epidemiological and mechanistic evidence suggests a protective role, especially against colorectal cancer, via inhibition of mitogenic prostaglandins and reduction of mucosal inflammation. Interventional RCTs are still needed to confirm direct clinical benefit.

6. Dietary Sources vs Supplementation

The choice between dietary sources and supplementation should consider bioavailability, safety (contaminants such as mercury and PCBs), cost, and convenience. Cold-water fatty fish remain the preferred source, as they provide EPA and DHA in the form of phospholipids and triglycerides, accompanied by synergistic micronutrients.

7. Recommended Doses and Safety

Dosage recommendations vary according to clinical objective and reference organization. The main guidelines establish the following parameters:

  • General prevention and cardiovascular health: 250–500mg EPA+DHA/day (American Heart Association; EFSA). Achievable with 2 servings of fatty fish per week.
  • Hypertriglyceridemia: 2–4g EPA+DHA/day, under medical prescription. Pure EPA formulation (icosapent ethyl) is preferred in this context, as DHA may modestly raise LDL cholesterol.
  • Adjunctive treatment for depression: 1–2g EPA/day, in addition to standard treatment.
  • Pregnant women: 200mg additional DHA/day (beyond dietary intake), as recommended by ISSFAL and EFSA.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: ≥2.7g EPA+DHA/day for at least 3 months.

From a safety standpoint, omega-3 has a favorable profile at recommended doses. Doses above 3g/day may modestly increase bleeding risk — relevant in patients taking warfarin, heparin, or antiplatelet agents. The FDA considers consumption of up to 3g/day of EPA+DHA from supplements to be safe, and up to 2g/day of pure EPA.

Lipid oxidation (rancidity) is the primary quality concern with fish oil capsules. Oxidized products may be pro-inflammatory and ineffective. The TOTOX index (Total Oxidation Value) should be below 26 meq/kg, and certification seals such as IFOS (International Fish Oil Standards) and NSF International guarantee purity and oxidative stability. Enteric-coated capsules reduce fishy-tasting burping.

8. Conclusion

Long-chain omega-3 fatty acids, particularly EPA and DHA, rank among the nutrients with the greatest accumulated scientific evidence for human health benefits. Their pleiotropic action — simultaneously modulating triglycerides, systemic inflammation, endothelial function, cardiac electrophysiology, mental health, and neuronal integrity — sets them apart from any other available dietary supplement.

The evidence-based approach prioritizes, as the first line, increasing consumption of cold-water fatty fish in the regular diet. Supplementation with certified quality fish oil is indicated when clinical needs exceed what food can provide — especially in hypertriglyceridemia, chronic inflammatory states, pregnancy, and as an adjunct in mental health.

Personalization is key: the erythrocyte omega-3 index (the proportion of EPA+DHA in red blood cells) offers an objective window into individual nutritional status and emerges as a biomarker of cardiovascular and cognitive risk, enabling more precise therapeutic decision-making. Consulting a physician or nutritionist for individualized dosing is always the safest and most effective path.
