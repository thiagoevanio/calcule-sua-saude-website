Muscle as a Gland

Historically seen only as the engine of locomotion, skeletal muscle is actually the largest endocrine organ in the human body. During contraction, it secretes hundreds of bioactive peptides (myokines) that travel through circulation to "talk" to the liver, adipose tissue, pancreas, and even the brain, orchestrating a systemic metabolic and anti-inflammatory response.

1. Introduction: The Paradigm Shift

For decades, physiology focused on the ability of exercise to burn calories and strengthen muscle fibers. However, a question persisted: how does physical exercise protect against diseases as disparate as type 2 diabetes, Alzheimer's, colon cancer, and depression?

The answer began to emerge in the early 2000s with the discovery that skeletal muscle, when contracted, releases signaling substances into the bloodstream. This discovery redefined sedentarism not just as a "lack of movement," but as a state of muscle endocrine deficiency, leading to metabolic dysfunction and chronic inflammation.

2. What Are Myokines?

Myokines are cytokines or other peptides that are expressed, produced, and released by muscle fibers, exerting autocrine, paracrine, or endocrine effects.

The muscle "secretome" is vast. Modern proteomics has identified over 600 potential myokines. Unlike classic hormones released by specialized glands, myokine secretion is dependent on the energy and mechanical demand of muscle contraction, creating a direct link between physical activity and homeostatic regulation.

3. Interleukin-6 (IL-6): The Prototypical One

IL-6 was the first myokine identified and remains the most studied. Interestingly, IL-6 is classically known as a pro-inflammatory cytokine (released by macrophages in sepsis). However, muscle-derived IL-6 has an opposite biological personality.

Characteristic Pro-Inflammatory IL-6 (Immune) Muscle IL-6 (Myokine)
Origin Macrophages / Adipocytes (visceral fat) Contracting myocytes
Signaling Activates NF-κB pathway (Inflammation) Independent of NF-κB, activates AMPK and GLP-1
Systemic Effect Insulin Resistance, elevated CRP Increased insulin sensitivity, fat oxidation
Context Sedentarism, Infection, Obesity Acute Physical Exercise

During exercise, plasma levels of IL-6 can increase up to 100-fold. It acts on the liver increasing gluconeogenesis (to feed the muscle) and on adipose tissue stimulating lipolysis. Most importantly, muscle IL-6 inhibits the production of TNF-α (tumor necrosis factor), generating a potent systemic anti-inflammatory effect.

4. Irisin and Adipose Tissue "Browning"

Discovered in 2012 by Harvard researchers, Irisin (named after the Greek goddess Iris, the messenger) is cleaved from the membrane protein FNDC5 in response to aerobic exercise and, to a lesser extent, resistance training.

Irisin's primary function is to induce the thermogenesis program in white adipose tissue. It stimulates the expression of uncoupling protein 1 (UCP1), transforming white adipocytes (energy storers) into "beige" or "brown" adipocytes (energy burners). This process, known as "Browning," dissipates chemical energy as heat, improving metabolic profile and protecting against obesity.

5. The Muscle-Brain Axis: BDNF and Cathepsin B

The old saying "sound mind in a sound body" now has a molecular basis. Exercise stimulates muscle production of Cathepsin B and Irisin, both capable of crossing the blood-brain barrier.

"In the brain, these muscle messengers induce the expression of BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor) in the hippocampus, promoting neurogenesis, synaptic plasticity, and protection against neurodegeneration."

BDNF acts like "fertilizer" for neurons. Low levels of BDNF are associated with depression, Alzheimer's, and cognitive decline, while exercise-induced increases improve memory and mood.

6. Myostatin and Decorin: Mass Regulators

Myostatin is a unique myokine because it acts as a negative regulator of muscle growth; it prevents muscles from growing excessively. Resistance exercise (weight training) suppresses Myostatin expression.

Concurrently, contracted muscle releases Decorin, a myokine that binds to circulating Myostatin and inhibits it. Additionally, contraction releases Follistatin, another Myostatin antagonist. This fine balance between promoters and inhibitors determines muscle hypertrophy and is crucial in combating sarcopenia (loss of muscle mass with age).

7. Crosstalk: The Conversation Between Organs

Myokines do not act in isolation; they form an integrated communication network:

  • Muscle-Liver: IL-6 and FGF-21 improve hepatic insulin sensitivity and reduce steatosis (fatty liver).
  • Muscle-Pancreas: IL-6 stimulates GLP-1 secretion by intestinal L cells, which in turn optimizes insulin secretion by pancreatic beta cells.
  • Muscle-Bone: Myokines like IGF-1 and Irisin have osteogenic effects, increasing bone mineral density. This explains why sarcopenia (muscle loss) is almost invariably accompanied by osteoporosis (bone loss).
  • Muscle-Tumor: Recent studies, such as those by Pernille Hojman, suggest that myokines (including IL-6, oncostatin M, and SPARC) activate Natural Killer (NK) cells, enhancing the immune system's ability to infiltrate and destroy tumors.

8. Exercise as Medicine

The discovery of myokines scientifically validates prescribing exercise as a first-line therapy. It's not just about "burning calories," but administering a dose of "endogenous drugs."

Unlike a pill that targets a single target (e.g., metformin in the liver), an exercise session releases a polypharmacological cocktail (IL-6, IL-15, Irisin, Meteorin-like, etc.) that targets multiple organs simultaneously, with positive side effects. The lack of circulating myokines in sedentary individuals creates a permissive environment for chronic low-grade inflammation ("Inflammaging"), accelerating biological aging.

9. Conclusion

Skeletal muscle is much more than a mechanical tool; it is a vital organ of metabolic and immunological regulation. Myokines represent the molecular basis by which movement promotes health. Maintaining muscle mass and regular physical activity is not an aesthetic issue, but an endocrine necessity to ensure proper communication between organs and functional longevity.
