Low Back Pain:
A Clinical Guide

Clinical Pearl — Non-Specific Pain is the Rule

Approximately 85% of low back pain episodes have no identifiable structural cause on imaging. This means that clinical examination is more important than immediately ordering an MRI, which frequently reveals irrelevant findings such as asymptomatic disc protrusions.

1. Epidemiology: Why Back Pain is Universal

Low back pain is one of the most prevalent health problems in human history. It is estimated that 80% of the world's population will experience at least one episode of lumbar pain during their lifetime, making it the leading cause of work absenteeism both globally and in most high-income countries. The aggregate economic cost — including lost workdays, medical expenses and reduced productivity — is estimated at hundreds of billions of dollars per year worldwide.

Prevalence increases progressively with age, peaking between 35 and 55 years, the period of greatest productive activity. The Global Burden of Disease Study (GBD 2019) reaffirmed low back pain as the leading cause of years lived with disability (YLDs) globally, surpassing conditions such as diabetes, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease and depression. Administrative data from national social security systems in multiple countries consistently place low back pain among the top causes of temporary disability benefits.

Despite its enormous prevalence, the prognosis for most acute episodes is favorable: approximately 60% of patients improve within six weeks with conservative management. However, around 10% progress to chronicity, which accounts for the majority of the economic and social burden associated with this condition.

2. Lumbar Spine Anatomy

Understanding the causes and treatment of low back pain requires a solid grasp of lumbar spine anatomy. The lumbar region comprises five vertebrae (L1–L5), the largest in the entire vertebral column, engineered to bear most of the body's weight while allowing wide range of motion in flexion, extension and rotation.

Between each vertebral pair lie the intervertebral discs — fibrocartilaginous structures composed of the nucleus pulposus (a central gelatinous core rich in water and proteoglycans) and the annulus fibrosus (concentric lamellae of collagen fibers). The discs function as hydraulic shock absorbers, distributing compressive loads. With aging, progressive dehydration of the nucleus pulposus reduces this capacity, predisposing to injury.

The intervertebral foramina are bilateral openings through which spinal nerve roots emerge. The L4, L5 and S1 roots are most frequently compromised in sciatica, as they pass through regions susceptible to disc compression and foraminal stenosis. The muscular stabilization system is divided into two main groups: the paraspinal muscles (erector spinae and multifidus), serving as primary stabilizers controlling segmental motion, and the core muscles (transversus abdominis, internal and external obliques, diaphragm and pelvic floor), which form a cylinder of intra-abdominal pressure essential for lumbar stability.

The ligamentous system — comprising the anterior and posterior longitudinal ligaments, the yellow ligament (ligamentum flavum), supraspinous and interspinous ligaments — provides passive stability. The normal lumbar lordotic curve represents a biomechanical adaptation that distributes forces efficiently; both excessive hyperlordosis and lumbar flattening alter this distribution and increase degenerative injury risk.

3. Types and Causes

Low back pain is classically classified by duration as: acute (less than 6 weeks), subacute (6 to 12 weeks) and chronic (more than 12 weeks). From an etiological standpoint, a distinction is made between specific low back pain (with an identifiable cause, ~15% of cases) and non-specific low back pain (~85%), in which no discrete structural lesion or pathology is identified as responsible for the symptoms.

Among the most clinically relevant specific causes:

  • Disc herniation: protrusion or extrusion of the nucleus pulposus through the annulus fibrosus, compressing an adjacent nerve root and causing radicular pain (sciatica), paresthesias and, in severe cases, motor deficit.
  • Lumbar spinal stenosis: progressive narrowing of the vertebral canal, common in elderly patients with advanced degenerative disease. Causes neurogenic claudication — pain and weakness in the lower limbs triggered by walking and relieved by trunk flexion.
  • Facet syndrome: osteoarthritis of the zygapophyseal (facet) joints, with mechanical pain exacerbated by spinal extension and rotation.
  • Spondylolisthesis: anterior slip of one vertebra over the one below, potentially compressing neural structures and causing segmental instability.
  • Fragility vertebral fracture: particularly in osteoporotic older women, occurring with minimal trauma.
  • Visceral causes (critical differential diagnosis): renal colic, pyelonephritis, abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) and gynecological pathology can present as low back pain. A ruptured AAA is a vascular emergency that may mimic acute severe lumbar pain.

The main risk factors for low back pain include: physical inactivity, obesity (especially visceral adiposity), heavy physical work with poor posture, whole-body vibration (professional drivers), and — increasingly recognized — psychosocial factors: pain catastrophizing, kinesiophobia (fear of movement), depression, anxiety and job dissatisfaction are more robust predictors of chronification than imaging findings.

"Non-specific low back pain is frequently a biopsychosocial experience, not merely a structural lesion — treating only the disc ignores most of the problem."

4. Red Flags

Identifying so-called "red flags" is essential in the initial assessment of any patient with low back pain. These signs indicate the possibility of a serious underlying cause requiring immediate investigation and specific treatment, differing radically from the management of non-specific low back pain.

Red Flag Possible Cause
Fever + rest pain (especially nocturnal) Vertebral infection (spondylodiscitis, epidural abscess)
Unexplained weight loss Malignancy (vertebral metastasis, multiple myeloma)
Progressive neurological deficit (weakness, numbness) Severe spinal cord compression, cauda equina syndrome
Urinary/fecal incontinence + saddle anesthesia Cauda equina syndrome — SURGICAL EMERGENCY
Recent trauma + intense lumbar pain Unstable vertebral fracture
Chronic corticosteroid use / known osteoporosis Fragility fracture
History of prior cancer Vertebral metastasis
Age > 50 years with new and intense pain Malignancy, abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA)

The absence of red flags allows conservative management of low back pain without the need for imaging in the acute phase. The presence of any of these signs, however, requires urgent diagnostic investigation.

5. Diagnosis

The diagnosis of low back pain is primarily clinical. A detailed history — pain characteristics, aggravating and relieving factors, radiation, associated symptoms — combined with a thorough physical examination including neurological testing (strength, reflexes, sensation, straight leg raise/Lasègue test) is sufficient to guide initial management in the vast majority of cases.

Imaging investigations have precise indications and should not be routinely ordered for acute non-specific low back pain:

  • Plain radiography (X-ray): indicated as the first investigation for pain associated with trauma or in patients over 50. Evaluates vertebral alignment, disc spaces, fractures and bony lesions.
  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI): gold standard for soft tissue assessment. Indicated when there is progressive neurological deficit, suspected infection or malignancy, or pain persisting beyond 6 weeks without improvement. Important caveat: population studies show that 50% of asymptomatic adults over 40 have disc protrusions on MRI — these are incidental findings that must not be treated as the cause of pain without rigorous clinical correlation.
  • Computed Tomography (CT): useful for bony structure assessment, fractures and foraminal stenosis; inferior to MRI for soft tissues and involves higher ionizing radiation dose.
  • Electroneuromyography (EMG): indicated to confirm and localize specific nerve compression, especially when diagnostic doubt exists or surgical planning is needed.
  • Laboratory tests: ESR, CRP, full blood count and alkaline phosphatase are requested when infectious or neoplastic processes are suspected.

Functional assessment can be complemented by validated instruments such as the Oswestry Disability Index (ODI) and the Roland-Morris Disability Questionnaire, which quantify the impact of pain on daily activities and guide monitoring of treatment response.

6. Conservative Treatment (First Line)

Contemporary scientific evidence is clear: conservative and multidisciplinary treatment is the first-line approach for the overwhelming majority of patients with low back pain. The most counterintuitive — and most evidence-supported — recommendation is to maintain physical activity and avoid prolonged bed rest, which worsens prognosis by promoting deconditioning, stiffness and avoidance behavior.

  • Physiotherapy: includes techniques such as Global Postural Re-education, the McKenzie Method (mechanical diagnosis and treatment of the spine based on pain centralization), segmental stabilization and manual therapy (vertebral mobilization and manipulation). The combination of active exercises and manual therapy holds grade A evidence for chronic low back pain.
  • Clinical Pilates: effective in strengthening the core and improving lumbar motor control, with growing evidence for reducing pain and functional disability.
  • Pain Neuroscience Education (PNE): a psychoeducational approach that teaches patients about the mechanisms of chronic pain, reducing catastrophizing and kinesiophobia — independent predictors of chronification and worse prognosis.
  • Pharmacotherapy: acetaminophen (first-line for mild to moderate pain), NSAIDs such as diclofenac and ibuprofen (effective for short periods in acute and inflammatory pain), muscle relaxants such as cyclobenzaprine and orphenadrine (for acute muscle spasm, use 5–7 days). Opioids should be avoided in non-specific chronic low back pain: risk of dependence consistently outweighs therapeutic benefit in systematic reviews.
  • Injections: epidural corticosteroid block and facet infiltration are options for selected cases with intense radicular pain refractory to conservative treatment, as a bridge to natural recovery or surgical decision.
  • Psychotherapy (Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy — CBT): fundamental in the management of chronic low back pain with central sensitization. Reduces catastrophizing, improves self-efficacy and functionality independently of pain intensity.

7. Prevention and Ergonomics

Preventing new episodes of low back pain is as important as treating the acute episode. Regular physical exercise is the preventive intervention with the best level of evidence: aerobic conditioning programs (walking, swimming, cycling) reduce recurrence of low back pain by 30 to 50% when practiced consistently.

Specific core strengthening with exercises such as the plank, bird-dog and dead bug improves segmental motor control and lumbar muscle endurance. These exercises should be progressive, initiated under physiotherapy supervision to ensure correct technique and avoid inappropriate overload.

Ergonomic measures include:

  • Sitting posture with the spine in a neutral position, feet flat on the floor and computer screen at eye level.
  • Use of an ergonomic chair with adjustable lumbar support and seat height.
  • Lifting heavy objects with knee flexion (squat position) and neutral spine, avoiding trunk flexion with extended knees.
  • Active breaks every 45–60 minutes of sedentary work, with stretching and short walks.
  • Medium-firm mattress: although evidence is limited, medium-firmness mattresses appear to offer better lumbar support than very soft or very firm ones.
  • Body weight management: each kilogram of excess weight increases load on the L5–S1 segment; weight loss in obese patients significantly reduces mechanical overload.

8. When to Consider Surgery

Surgery is indicated in a minority of low back pain patients, generally those with specific and well-defined causes who have not responded to conservative treatment. Absolute and relative indications include:

  • Cauda equina syndrome (EMERGENCY): urinary/fecal retention or incontinence, saddle anesthesia and perineal neurological deficit require surgical decompression within less than 24 hours to preserve sphincteric function.
  • Progressive neurological deficit: progressive motor weakness in the lower limb, regardless of duration, indicates surgical decompression.
  • Disc herniation with disabling radicular pain for 6 to 12 weeks without improvement with adequate conservative treatment.
  • Spinal canal stenosis with severe and refractory neurogenic claudication to conservative treatment for at least 3–6 months.

It is essential to inform patients about realistic expectations. The SPORT trial (Spine Patient Outcomes Research Trial) demonstrated that for symptomatic disc herniation, surgery provides faster improvement in pain and function in the first months, but 2- and 4-year outcomes are similar between surgical and conservative groups. This reinforces that the surgical decision should be shared, considering patient preferences, functional impact and failure of optimized conservative treatment.

9. Conclusion

Low back pain is one of the most prevalent and impactful health conditions of the modern world, requiring an approach that transcends the purely biomechanical model. Current understanding recognizes lumbar pain as a biopsychosocial phenomenon, in which anatomical, neurophysiological, psychological and social factors interact in complex ways to determine the pain experience, functional disability and risk of chronification.

The vast majority of episodes — especially acute and subacute ones — respond favorably to evidence-based conservative treatment: maintenance of physical activity, targeted physiotherapy, pain neuroscience education and judicious medication use. Imaging investigation should be reserved for clinically justified situations, avoiding overdiagnosis and unnecessary treatment of incidental findings.

Prevention, through regular exercise, weight management and ergonomic measures, is the most efficient path to reducing the individual and collective burden of this condition. For cases with red flags, progressive neurological deficit or cauda equina syndrome, timely surgical response can be decisive. A multidisciplinary approach — uniting physiotherapist, physician, psychologist and exercise specialist — represents the most advanced and effective standard of care available.
